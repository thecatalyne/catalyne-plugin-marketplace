# Professional Brand Frameworks

Reference for established brand strategy frameworks used in professional brand identity work. These frameworks structure thinking about brand positioning, purpose, and strategy — they complement the technique library by providing theoretical grounding.

## How to Use This Reference

- **During positioning discovery**: When a technique references a framework (Wolff Olins Butterfly, competitive mapping), the detail is here.
- **During synthesis**: Frameworks help structure the analyst's comparison and identify what's strong vs. missing.
- **During build**: Design principles should trace back to strategic positioning — these frameworks provide that link.

---

## 1. Wolff Olins Butterfly Model

**Origin**: Wolff Olins, one of the world's leading brand consultancies (Uber, Google, Tate)

### The Model

The brand lives at the intersection of two questions:

```
   What's special        What does the
    about you?        world actually need?
         \                  /
          \                /
           \    BRAND     /
            \   LIVES    /
             \  HERE    /
              \        /
               \      /
                \    /
                 \  /
                  \/
```

- **Left wing — Internal truth**: Your unique capabilities, culture, vision, what you can do that nobody else can
- **Right wing — External need**: Market gaps, customer pain, cultural shifts, what the world is asking for
- **Center — Brand positioning**: The overlap where your unique strengths meet real-world demand

### Why It Matters

- Only left wing = navel-gazing (building what you love, not what anyone needs)
- Only right wing = commodity (solving a real problem, but anyone could)
- Both wings = distinctive, purposeful brand position

### Application in Discovery

The Butterfly technique in the technique library walks through both wings. Key outputs:
- `butterfly.special`: What's genuinely unique about this brand
- `butterfly.world_needs`: What external need it addresses
- The positioning statement synthesizes both

### Diagnostic Questions

- "If you disappeared, could someone else do exactly what you do?" (If yes, left wing is weak)
- "Is the problem you solve getting more urgent or less?" (If less, right wing may be weak)
- "Can you explain the connection between what you're good at and what people need in one sentence?" (If not, the center isn't clear)

## 2. Aaker Brand Equity Model

**Origin**: David Aaker, UC Berkeley professor, widely considered the father of modern branding

### The Model

Brand equity consists of five assets:

1. **Brand Awareness**: Recognition and recall — can people name you?
2. **Perceived Quality**: Subjective quality assessment — do people trust your quality?
3. **Brand Associations**: Mental connections — what do people think of when they hear your name?
4. **Brand Loyalty**: Repeat behavior — do people come back?
5. **Proprietary Assets**: Patents, trademarks, channel relationships — what's legally/structurally yours?

### Application in This Plugin

The design system work primarily affects **Brand Associations** (visual and verbal identity shapes mental connections) and **Perceived Quality** (professional, coherent design signals quality). Discovery work maps the desired associations; the design system makes them tangible.

### Relevance to Discovery

When exploring brand personality, associations are what you're building:
- Archetype selection → desired character associations
- Color preferences → mood and category associations
- Voice → personality associations

Ask: "When someone encounters your brand for the first time, what three things should they associate with you?"

## 3. Jennifer Aaker's Brand Personality Framework

**Origin**: Jennifer Aaker (David Aaker's daughter), Stanford GSB, 1997

### The Model

See `personality-dimensions.md` for the full detailed reference. Summary:

Five dimensions of brand personality, each measurable on a 1-10 scale:
1. **Sincerity** — honest, wholesome, cheerful, down-to-earth
2. **Excitement** — daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date
3. **Competence** — reliable, intelligent, successful
4. **Sophistication** — upper class, charming, glamorous
5. **Ruggedness** — outdoorsy, tough, strong

### Why It's The Most-Used Framework

- **Measurable**: Unlike most brand personality tools, this produces numbers you can compare across team members
- **Validated**: Tested across cultures and categories with consistent factor structure
- **Actionable**: Each dimension maps directly to design and voice decisions (see personality-dimensions.md)

### Limitations

- Western-centric: The five factors were derived from American data. Some cultures add dimensions (e.g., "peacefulness" in Japan)
- Doesn't capture purpose: Tells you *how* the brand feels, not *why* it exists
- Can produce bland middle scores: If everything is 5-6, the profile isn't distinctive

## 4. Simon Sinek's Golden Circle

**Origin**: Simon Sinek, 2009 TED talk / "Start With Why"

### The Model

Three concentric circles, from inside out:

1. **Why** (core): Purpose — why does the brand exist beyond making money?
2. **How** (middle): Process — how does the brand fulfill its purpose?
3. **What** (outer): Product — what does the brand actually sell?

### Application in Discovery

Most people start with What and struggle with Why. The plugin's positioning techniques (Brand Eulogy, Wolff Olins Butterfly) are designed to access Why indirectly.

**Useful prompt**: "Every brand sells something (What). Some brands have a unique process (How). Very few brands are driven by a belief about how the world should work (Why). What's yours?"

**Warning**: "Why" can become fluffy ("We believe in making the world better"). Push for specificity: "Better how? For whom? Why does that matter to you personally?"

## 5. Marty Neumeier's Brand Gap

**Origin**: Marty Neumeier, "The Brand Gap" (2003) and "Zag" (2006)

### Core Idea

A brand is not what *you* say it is. It's what *they* say it is. The "brand gap" is the distance between your intended brand (strategy) and perceived brand (customer experience).

### Five Disciplines of Branding

1. **Differentiate**: Stand out from competitors on a meaningful dimension
2. **Collaborate**: Brand is cross-functional — not just marketing's job
3. **Innovate**: Brands that stop evolving die
4. **Validate**: Test with real people, not just internal consensus
5. **Cultivate**: Brand is a living system that needs ongoing care

### Relevance to This Plugin

The multi-stakeholder flow (multiple founders doing discovery independently) is an implementation of Neumeier's "Collaborate" principle — brand identity should reflect the whole team's vision, not just one person's.

The synthesis phase helps close the brand gap between different team members' perceptions.

### The "Zag" Test

From Neumeier's "Zag": When everyone zigs, zag.

**Question for discovery**: "In your industry, what does everyone do? What's the standard playbook? Now — where do you do the opposite?"

This is essentially competitive differentiation through inversion. The most powerful brand positions are defined by what they *don't* do as much as what they do.

## 6. Paul Rand's Design Principles

**Origin**: Paul Rand, designer of IBM, UPS, ABC, Westinghouse logos

### Principles Applied to Brand Identity

Rand's philosophy wasn't about brand strategy — it was about visual execution. But his principles inform design system quality:

1. **Simplicity**: Reduce until removing anything would break it
2. **Wit**: Intelligence expressed through restraint, not ornamentation
3. **Universality**: Works across contexts without modification
4. **Timelessness**: Resists dating by avoiding trends
5. **Memorability**: Sticks in the mind after one encounter

See `scoring-criteria.md` for Rand's 7 criteria adapted into a scoring framework.

### Application in Build Phase

When generating the design system, every element should pass the Rand filter:
- Is this the simplest version that works?
- Would this feel dated in 5 years?
- Can someone describe this from memory?

## 7. Pentagram's Systematic Identity Approach

**Origin**: Pentagram, the world's largest independent design consultancy

### Philosophy

Pentagram treats brand identity as a *system*, not a *logo*. The identity is the set of rules that generate consistent expression across every touchpoint.

### System Components

1. **Constants**: Things that never change (name, core mark, primary colors)
2. **Variables**: Things that flex (photography style, illustration, secondary colors, layout)
3. **Rules**: How constants and variables interact (spacing, hierarchy, composition)

### Application to This Plugin

The design system output should distinguish:
- **Core tokens** (constants): Primary palette, primary typeface, core spacing scale
- **Extended tokens** (variables): Secondary colors, accent typefaces, illustration style
- **Principles** (rules): How the system elements work together

This maps directly to the 3-layer token architecture in `token-architecture.md`:
- Primitive tokens = constants
- Semantic tokens = how constants apply to contexts
- Component tokens = specific UI applications

## 8. Minimum Viable Brand (Wolff Olins)

**Origin**: Wolff Olins, applied to startup contexts

### Core Idea

A Minimum Viable Brand (MVB) is **"harvested" from the product itself** — not designed in isolation, but extracted from what already exists. The artifacts it produces should be both powerful and useful, not just beautiful.

For startups, Wolff Olins advocates that brand artifacts should be working tools: manifestos that double as website copy, design tokens that ship in the product, voice guidelines that are the actual editorial standards.

### When to Use Guidelines vs. a Design System

| Situation | What You Need |
|-----------|---------------|
| Team under 5-10 people, single product/platform | **Brand guidelines** — logo usage, 2-4 colors, 1-2 typefaces, voice basics, do's and don'ts |
| Multiple designers/developers on the same product | **Design system** — tokens, components, coded references |
| Consistency breaking down across surfaces | **Design system** — the guidelines aren't enough if they aren't enforced through code |
| Dark mode, theming, or multi-brand support needed | **Design system** — this can't be done with a PDF |

### Minimum Viable Brand Guidelines (The Floor)

At minimum, every startup needs:
1. Logo with usage rules (don't stretch, minimum size, clear space)
2. **2-4 brand colors** with hex and RGB values
3. 1-2 accessible fonts with hierarchy
4. Basic voice and tone guidance
5. Visual do's and don'ts (prohibition examples)

This is the floor, not the ceiling. The plugin's full pipeline produces much more — but for users who need to ship fast, knowing the MVB floor is valuable.

### Application in This Plugin

The plugin's four phases naturally map to the MVB approach:
- **Discover** harvests brand identity from the founders' minds (not generated abstractly)
- **Synthesize** distills team input into actionable consensus
- **Build** produces the MVB at minimum, a full system at best
- **Export** creates artifacts that are working tools, not gallery pieces

When working with solo founders or very early-stage teams, frame the plugin output as "your Minimum Viable Brand — a real, usable system you can ship with today and evolve over time."

## Framework Selection Guide

| If the person is... | Recommend these frameworks |
|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Purpose-driven | Wolff Olins Butterfly, Golden Circle |
| Competitive-focused | Neumeier's Zag, Competitive Mapping |
| Analytical | Aaker Personality, Brand Equity |
| Visual/design-oriented | Rand's Principles, Pentagram's Systematic Approach |
| New to branding | Golden Circle (accessible), Archetypes (intuitive) |
| Experienced | Aaker Personality + Wolff Olins (more nuanced) |

These frameworks are tools, not dogma. Mix and match based on what produces the most useful signal for the specific brand.
