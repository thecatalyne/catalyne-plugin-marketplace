# Trap Navigation Guide

Common pitfalls in brand elicitation and how to handle them. The interviewer agent should recognize these patterns in real-time and navigate around them.

## How to Use This Reference

The interviewer agent loads this reference at the start of every discovery session. Each trap includes: what it looks like, why it happens, and specific phrases to redirect.

---

## 1. The Aspiration Trap

**What it looks like**: The person describes who they want to be, not who they are.
- "We're like Apple" (when they're a 3-person startup)
- Picking the Magician archetype because it sounds impressive
- Giving every Aaker dimension a 7-8 (wanting to be everything)

**Why it happens**: Brand identity feels aspirational. People naturally reach for idealized versions.

**How to navigate**:
- "That's a great aspiration. Is that where you are today, or where you're heading? Both are useful — they inform different decisions."
- "If a customer met you today, would they describe you that way? What might they say instead?"
- "Let's separate the now-brand from the future-brand. We can design a system that grows from one to the other."

**Key principle**: Capture both. Record the aspirational answer AND probe for the authentic current state. The gap between them is actionable — the design system can be positioned to bridge it.

---

## 2. The Consensus Trap

**What it looks like**: The person gives answers they think the rest of the team would agree with, rather than their own genuine perspective.
- "Well, we've always said we're..."
- "I think everyone would agree that..."
- Hedging on strong preferences

**Why it happens**: Fear of creating conflict. Wanting to be a team player. Prior team discussions creating anchoring.

**How to navigate**:
- "For this session, I only want YOUR perspective. The synthesis step is specifically designed to compare different viewpoints — that's where alignment happens."
- "If you could design this brand entirely by yourself, with no one to negotiate with, what would you do?"
- "You said 'we've always said...' — is that what you actually believe, or what's been agreed? Both are valuable but they're different data points."

**Key principle**: Discovery is per-person for exactly this reason. Reinforce that divergence is expected and productive.

---

## 3. The Indecision Trap

**What it looks like**: "I don't know," "I'm not sure," "maybe?" to most questions. The person can't commit to preferences.

**Why it happens**: Design preferences feel high-stakes ("what if I pick wrong?"). Non-designers may feel unqualified to have opinions.

**How to navigate**:
- Switch from generative to recognition techniques. Instead of "what color?" use palette recognition: "which of these resonates?"
- Lower the stakes: "There are no wrong answers here. We're mapping your instincts, not making final decisions."
- Use elimination: "You might not know what you want, but you probably know what you don't want. Let's start there."
- Use metaphor: "If your brand were a restaurant, what kind?" (People have opinions about restaurants even when they don't about design.)

**Key principle**: If someone can't generate, let them recognize. If they can't recognize, let them eliminate. There's always a path.

---

## 4. The Overthinking Trap

**What it looks like**: Long pauses, qualifying every answer, "well, it depends on the context," analyzing every option to paralysis.

**Why it happens**: Analytical people want to give the "right" answer. They see complexity that others don't.

**How to navigate**:
- "I love the nuance, but give me your gut reaction first. We'll layer in context after."
- "What if you had to decide in 3 seconds? Don't think, just pick."
- Lean into quantitative techniques (Aaker sliders, formality spectrum) — they give analytical people a framework that feels rigorous.
- Break decisions smaller: "Don't pick the palette. Just tell me — warm or cool?"

**Key principle**: Capture both the gut reaction and the analytical nuance. The gut reaction is personality data; the analysis is strategic data. Both are valuable.

---

## 5. The Kitchen Sink Trap

**What it looks like**: "We want to be innovative AND traditional. Bold AND subtle. Fun AND serious." Everything is important. No tradeoffs acknowledged.

**Why it happens**: Fear of limiting the brand. Wanting to appeal to everyone. Not understanding that brand is as much about what you're NOT as what you are.

**How to navigate**:
- "Every brand makes tradeoffs. Apple chose simplicity over customization. Nike chose boldness over subtlety. What are you willing to give up?"
- "If you could only be known for one thing, what would it be?"
- Use forced ranking: "You said innovative, bold, and warm. Rank them 1-2-3. If you could only keep one, which survives?"
- "Who is NOT your customer? Who would look at your brand and say 'this isn't for me'? That's healthy."

**Key principle**: Brand is differentiation. A brand that tries to be everything is nothing. Push (gently) for tradeoffs.

---

## 6. The Jargon Trap

**What it looks like**: "We want to leverage our synergistic value proposition to disrupt the..." Marketing speak instead of genuine expression.

**Why it happens**: People default to business language when talking about their brand. It feels "professional."

**How to navigate**:
- "Forget the pitch deck for a second. How would you explain this to a friend over coffee?"
- "What would a customer say about you? Not in a testimonial — in a real conversation with a friend."
- "Ban the word [jargon term] for the rest of this session. Now how do you say the same thing?"

**Key principle**: Jargon hides meaning. The goal is to surface what's underneath. Keep redirecting to plain language until genuine personality emerges.

---

## 7. The Comparison Trap

**What it looks like**: Every answer is relative to another brand. "We're like Airbnb but for X." "We're the Tesla of Y." No self-referential identity.

**Why it happens**: Comparison is a mental shortcut. It's easier to describe something relative to something known than to articulate it from scratch.

**How to navigate**:
- Accept comparisons initially — they're useful starting data. But then pivot: "What's the part of Airbnb that maps to you? And what's the part that doesn't?"
- "You've told me what you're like. Now tell me what you're unlike. Where does the comparison break down?"
- "If Airbnb didn't exist, how would you describe yourself? Start from zero."

**Key principle**: Comparisons are a starting point, not a destination. They help identify territory, but the brand needs to stand on its own description.

---

## 8. The Visual Intimidation Trap

**What it looks like**: "I'm not a designer, I can't answer visual questions." Deflecting all visual preference questions.

**Why it happens**: Visual design feels like a specialized skill. Non-designers think they need expertise to have opinions.

**How to navigate**:
- "You don't need to be a designer. You just need eyes and opinions. When you walk into a room, you know if it feels right. That's what we're doing here."
- Always use recognition mode: "I'm not asking you to design anything. Just tell me which of these you'd hang on your wall."
- Start with familiar territory: "Think of a space you love being in — a cafe, a home, a store. What does it look like? That's visual taste."
- If offering image gen prompts: "I can create some visual examples so you can react to something concrete rather than describing it from scratch."

**Key principle**: Everyone has visual preferences. The interviewer's job is to access them through comfortable channels, not to demand design vocabulary.

---

## 9. The Perfectionism Trap

**What it looks like**: "Can I change my answer?" "Actually, let me rethink that." Revising every response.

**Why it happens**: The person wants to give the perfect answer. They treat discovery like a test.

**How to navigate**:
- "Your first instinct is data. Your revision is also data. I'm capturing both."
- "Nothing here is permanent. This is discovery, not commitment. The design system is where we commit."
- "Think of this as a conversation, not a questionnaire. We're exploring, not deciding."

**Key principle**: Capture both the first instinct and the revision. The gap between them is information about what the person thinks they *should* want vs. what they actually want.

---

## 10. The Scope Creep Trap

**What it looks like**: "Wait, should we also talk about our product strategy?" "What about our investor deck?" The session expands beyond brand identity into business strategy.

**Why it happens**: Brand identity touches everything. It's natural to see connections to other domains.

**How to navigate**:
- "Great instinct — that's connected. Let's capture that thought and stay on brand identity for now. We can circle back after."
- Record in `raw_notes` — side observations are valuable even if they're out of scope.
- "We're building the foundation. The brand identity will inform all of those things, but let's nail the identity first."

**Key principle**: Stay focused on the discovery dimensions (positioning, personality, visual, voice). Note everything else but don't pursue it in-session.

---

## Meta-Pattern: Reading the Room

The most important interviewer skill is recognizing which trap is happening and adapting in real-time:

| Signal | Likely Trap | Recommended Shift |
|--------|------------|-------------------|
| Rehearsed-sounding answers | Jargon or Aspiration | Switch to metaphorical/playful techniques |
| "I don't know" repeatedly | Indecision or Intimidation | Switch to recognition or elimination techniques |
| Everything scored 5-7 | Kitchen Sink or Consensus | Force ranking, probe for tradeoffs |
| Long pauses, qualifiers | Overthinking | Switch to gut-reaction exercises, time-pressure |
| Constant revisions | Perfectionism | Reassure, capture both versions |
| "We always say..." | Consensus | Redirect to personal perspective |
| "We're like [brand]" | Comparison | Accept, then push for differentiation |
| "I'm not a designer" | Visual Intimidation | Switch to recognition mode, lower stakes |
| Expanding to strategy | Scope Creep | Capture in notes, redirect to identity |

**When in doubt**: Switch techniques. If one approach isn't working, try a different cognitive channel. The technique library exists precisely for this flexibility.
