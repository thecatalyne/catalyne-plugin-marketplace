# Brand Archetype Profiles

Based on Carl Jung's 12 universal archetypes, adapted for brand identity work. Each archetype represents a fundamental human motivation — brands that align with an archetype tap into deep, intuitive recognition.

## How to Use This Reference

- **During discovery**: Present 3-4 archetypes that seem relevant based on earlier conversation. Let the person read the profiles and identify which resonates.
- **Primary + Secondary**: Most brands are a blend. Pick a primary (dominant energy) and secondary (complementary texture).
- **Avoid forcing**: If none feel right, that's signal too. Explore why.

## The 12 Archetypes

### 1. The Innocent

**Core desire**: Safety, happiness, simplicity
**Brand promise**: Life can be simple and good
**Voice**: Optimistic, honest, humble, pure
**Visual associations**: Clean lines, light palettes, white space, natural imagery, soft edges

**Strengths**: Trust, optimism, clarity
**Risks**: Can seem naive, boring, or in denial about complexity

**Brand examples**: Dove, Coca-Cola (classic era), Whole Foods, Aveeno
**Best for**: Brands built on simplicity, purity, nostalgia, or wholesome values

**Key question**: "Does your brand believe the world is fundamentally good, and want to help people return to that simplicity?"

---

### 2. The Sage

**Core desire**: Truth, understanding, wisdom
**Brand promise**: We'll help you understand the world
**Voice**: Knowledgeable, articulate, thoughtful, sometimes academic
**Visual associations**: Clean typography, structured layouts, muted/scholarly palettes, information-dense design

**Strengths**: Expertise, credibility, depth of knowledge
**Risks**: Can seem cold, elitist, overly intellectual, analysis paralysis

**Brand examples**: Google, BBC, MIT, The Economist, McKinsey
**Best for**: Brands built on expertise, research, education, or informed decision-making

**Key question**: "Does your brand exist to help people think more clearly or understand something deeply?"

---

### 3. The Explorer

**Core desire**: Freedom, discovery, self-sufficiency
**Brand promise**: Don't fence me in
**Voice**: Independent, adventurous, pioneering, restless
**Visual associations**: Wide landscapes, earth tones, rugged textures, expansive compositions, natural materials

**Strengths**: Ambition, authenticity, independence
**Risks**: Can seem aimless, unable to commit, alienating to homebodies

**Brand examples**: Patagonia, Jeep, REI, National Geographic, Subaru
**Best for**: Brands built on adventure, discovery, pushing boundaries, or self-reliance

**Key question**: "Does your brand help people break free from the ordinary and find their own path?"

---

### 4. The Outlaw (Rebel)

**Core desire**: Liberation, revolution, breaking rules
**Brand promise**: Rules are made to be broken
**Voice**: Disruptive, bold, provocative, countercultural
**Visual associations**: Dark palettes, high contrast, distressed textures, unconventional layouts, sharp angles

**Strengths**: Courage to challenge, radical loyalty from fans, cultural impact
**Risks**: Can seem destructive, irresponsible, or performatively edgy

**Brand examples**: Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Diesel, Liquid Death, Cards Against Humanity
**Best for**: Brands that exist to challenge conventions, empower outsiders, or disrupt industries

**Key question**: "Does your brand exist because something about the status quo is broken, and you're here to tear it down?"

---

### 5. The Magician

**Core desire**: Transformation, making dreams real
**Brand promise**: We make the impossible possible
**Voice**: Visionary, imaginative, charismatic, transformative
**Visual associations**: Rich/deep color palettes, gradients, dynamic motion, polished surfaces, surprise elements

**Strengths**: Innovation, vision, ability to inspire wonder
**Risks**: Can seem manipulative, over-promising, disconnected from reality

**Brand examples**: Apple, Disney, Tesla, Dyson, SpaceX
**Best for**: Brands built on transformative experiences, innovation, or turning vision into reality

**Key question**: "Does your brand promise to transform how people experience something fundamental?"

---

### 6. The Hero

**Core desire**: Mastery, courage, proving worth through action
**Brand promise**: Where there's a will, there's a way
**Voice**: Confident, determined, inspiring, action-oriented
**Visual associations**: Bold colors, strong typography, dynamic composition, sharp angles, high energy

**Strengths**: Discipline, courage, competence, inspiration
**Risks**: Can seem arrogant, combative, or exhausting

**Brand examples**: Nike, FedEx, BMW, Under Armour, Gatorade
**Best for**: Brands built on performance, achievement, overcoming obstacles, or empowering action

**Key question**: "Does your brand help people rise to a challenge and prove what they're capable of?"

---

### 7. The Lover

**Core desire**: Intimacy, connection, sensory experience
**Brand promise**: You are worthy of desire
**Voice**: Passionate, sensual, warm, intimate, appreciative
**Visual associations**: Warm palettes, rich textures, flowing forms, luxurious materials, close-up photography

**Strengths**: Passion, appreciation, commitment to beauty and connection
**Risks**: Can seem superficial, manipulative through desire, or exclusionary

**Brand examples**: Chanel, Victoria's Secret, Godiva, Alfa Romeo, Nespresso
**Best for**: Brands built on beauty, pleasure, intimacy, or elevated sensory experiences

**Key question**: "Does your brand make people feel more attractive, connected, or alive in their senses?"

---

### 8. The Jester

**Core desire**: Joy, fun, lightness, living in the moment
**Brand promise**: If you can't have fun, what's the point?
**Voice**: Playful, irreverent, witty, sometimes absurd
**Visual associations**: Bright/saturated colors, rounded shapes, playful typography, unexpected combinations, cartoon-like elements

**Strengths**: Joy, originality, ability to reframe and disarm
**Risks**: Can seem unserious, frivolous, unable to deliver on substance

**Brand examples**: Old Spice, M&M's, Ben & Jerry's, Dollar Shave Club, Mailchimp
**Best for**: Brands built on fun, humor, or making traditionally boring things enjoyable

**Key question**: "Does your brand believe that joy and humor are the best way to connect with people?"

---

### 9. The Everyperson (Regular Guy/Gal)

**Core desire**: Belonging, connection, fitting in
**Brand promise**: We're all in this together
**Voice**: Friendly, down-to-earth, empathetic, unpretentious
**Visual associations**: Warm neutrals, approachable typography, inclusive imagery, comfortable compositions, familiar patterns

**Strengths**: Empathy, realism, lack of pretension, broad relatability
**Risks**: Can seem bland, forgettable, lacking a point of view

**Brand examples**: IKEA, Target, Budweiser, Gap, Slack
**Best for**: Brands built on accessibility, community, or making good things available to everyone

**Key question**: "Does your brand succeed by being genuinely accessible and making people feel like they belong?"

---

### 10. The Caregiver

**Core desire**: Protecting and caring for others
**Brand promise**: We'll take care of you
**Voice**: Nurturing, warm, reassuring, generous, selfless
**Visual associations**: Soft palettes, rounded shapes, warm tones, gentle gradients, organic forms

**Strengths**: Generosity, compassion, consistency, trust
**Risks**: Can seem martyrish, patronizing, or enabling dependency

**Brand examples**: Johnson & Johnson, TOMS, Volvo, Campbell's, UNICEF
**Best for**: Brands built on service, protection, health, or nurturing growth in others

**Key question**: "Does your brand exist fundamentally to take care of someone or protect them from harm?"

---

### 11. The Ruler

**Core desire**: Control, order, creating prosperity through structure
**Brand promise**: Power isn't everything — it's the only thing
**Voice**: Commanding, authoritative, confident, structured, exclusive
**Visual associations**: Dark/rich palettes, serif typography, symmetrical layouts, premium materials, traditional elegance

**Strengths**: Leadership, responsibility, order, quality standards
**Risks**: Can seem authoritarian, out of touch, rigid, or elitist

**Brand examples**: Mercedes-Benz, Rolex, Microsoft, American Express, Louis Vuitton
**Best for**: Brands built on leadership, premium quality, security, or institutional trust

**Key question**: "Does your brand create order, set standards, or offer the definitive version of something?"

---

### 12. The Creator

**Core desire**: Innovation, self-expression, making something that endures
**Brand promise**: If you can imagine it, it can be made
**Voice**: Inventive, expressive, perfectionist, non-conforming, visionary
**Visual associations**: Unique/distinctive palettes, creative typography, asymmetrical layouts, handcrafted textures, artistic compositions

**Strengths**: Creativity, vision, authenticity, ability to inspire
**Risks**: Can seem perfectionist to the point of impracticality, self-indulgent, or elitist

**Brand examples**: Adobe, Lego, Moleskine, Crayola, Pinterest
**Best for**: Brands built on creativity, craft, tools for makers, or self-expression

**Key question**: "Does your brand exist to help people make something meaningful that didn't exist before?"

## Archetype Blends

Most brands combine a primary and secondary archetype. Common pairings:

| Primary | Common Secondaries | Blend Character |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Sage | Creator | Thoughtful innovation (think: Notion) |
| Hero | Explorer | Ambitious adventure (think: North Face) |
| Magician | Creator | Visionary craft (think: Apple) |
| Outlaw | Jester | Irreverent disruption (think: Liquid Death) |
| Caregiver | Innocent | Gentle nurturing (think: Dove) |
| Ruler | Sage | Authoritative expertise (think: The Economist) |
| Creator | Explorer | Pioneering expression (think: GoPro) |
| Everyperson | Jester | Relatable fun (think: Mailchimp) |
| Lover | Creator | Beautiful craft (think: Chanel) |

**Tension blends** (opposing energies, harder to execute but distinctive):
- Outlaw + Caregiver: "We break rules to protect the vulnerable"
- Sage + Jester: "We teach through humor"
- Ruler + Explorer: "We set the standard for frontier"

## Facilitation Tips

1. **Don't read all 12 aloud.** Present 3-4 that seem relevant based on earlier conversation. If none land, try 3-4 more.
2. **Use the key questions.** They're faster than reading full profiles.
3. **"None of these" is useful data.** Ask what's missing — it often reveals a blend or an underrepresented angle.
4. **Watch for aspirational vs. authentic.** People often pick what they want to be, not what they are. Gently probe: "Is that who you are today, or who you want to become?"
5. **Secondary matters.** The primary sets the dominant energy; the secondary adds nuance and prevents cliche.
